Phase 0: Essential Foundation

Build the mathematical and programming bedrock

⏱️ 4–8 weeks ⭐ Beginner

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Master single & multivariable calculus fundamentals
  • Understand linear algebra: vectors, matrices, eigenvalues
  • Write Python programs using NumPy and Matplotlib
  • Solve basic ODEs numerically (Euler method)
  • Apply Newton's laws to simulate physical systems
📐 Mathematics
  • Calculus: Limits, derivatives, integrals, Taylor series
  • Linear Algebra: Vectors, matrices, eigenvalues, SVD
  • Probability: Distributions, expectation, CLT
  • ODEs: First & second order, initial value problems
💡 Why: Every numerical method is built on calculus and linear algebra. Taylor series underpin finite differences.
⚡ Physics
  • Classical Mechanics: Newton's laws, Lagrangian, oscillations
  • E&M: Maxwell's equations, potentials, boundary conditions
  • Statistical Physics: Ensembles, partition function
  • Waves: Wave equation, superposition, boundary conditions
💡 Why: You need physical intuition to know what to simulate and how to validate results.
🐍 Python & Scientific Stack
  • Core Python: Loops, functions, classes, file I/O
  • NumPy: Arrays, broadcasting, vectorized operations
  • SciPy: Optimization, integration, interpolation
  • Matplotlib: 2D plots, subplots, customization
💡 Why: Python is the lingua franca of computational physics. NumPy vectorization is 100x faster than loops.

Phase 1: Numerical Toolbox

Master the core algorithms every computational physicist needs

⏱️ 4–6 weeks ⭐⭐ Intermediate

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Implement numerical integration (trapezoidal, Simpson's, Gaussian)
  • Solve ODEs with RK4 and symplectic (Verlet) integrators
  • Find roots using bisection, Newton-Raphson, secant methods
  • Solve linear systems and eigenvalue problems numerically
  • Understand error analysis and convergence rates
∫ Integration & Differentiation
  • Trapezoidal rule & error O(h²)
  • Simpson's rule & error O(h⁴)
  • Gaussian quadrature (optimal node placement)
  • Finite differences: forward, backward, central
  • Richardson extrapolation for higher accuracy
💡 Why: Integration appears in expectation values, partition functions, and solving integral equations.
🔄 ODE Solvers
  • Euler method (1st order, unstable for oscillators)
  • RK2 (midpoint method)
  • RK4 (classic 4th-order Runge-Kutta)
  • Verlet integration (symplectic, energy-conserving)
  • Adaptive step-size: RK45 (Dormand-Prince)
💡 Why: Most physics problems reduce to ODEs. Choosing the right integrator determines whether your simulation is stable and accurate.
🎯 Root Finding & Optimization
  • Bisection method (guaranteed convergence)
  • Newton-Raphson (fast, needs derivative)
  • Secant method (no derivative needed)
  • scipy.optimize: minimize, root_scalar, curve_fit
  • Gradient descent basics
💡 Why: Finding energy minima, equilibrium points, and fitting models to data all require root finding.
📊 Linear Algebra Computations
  • Direct solvers: LU decomposition, np.linalg.solve
  • Eigenvalue problems: np.linalg.eig
  • Sparse matrices (scipy.sparse)
  • Condition numbers and numerical stability
  • Iterative methods: Jacobi, Gauss-Seidel
💡 Why: Quantum mechanics, normal modes, and PDE discretizations all lead to matrix eigenvalue problems.

Phase 2: Core Physics Simulations

Simulate chaos, PDEs, and quantum mechanics

⏱️ 6–8 weeks ⭐⭐⭐ Intermediate+

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Simulate chaotic systems and compute Lyapunov exponents
  • Solve PDEs using finite difference methods
  • Implement the Crank-Nicolson scheme for diffusion
  • Solve the time-independent Schrödinger equation numerically
  • Simulate quantum wave packet dynamics
🌀 Classical & Celestial Mechanics
  • Double pendulum: chaos and sensitivity to ICs
  • Three-body problem: figure-8, chaotic orbits
  • Lyapunov exponents: quantifying chaos
  • Poincaré sections and phase space portraits
  • Symplectic integrators for long-term stability
💡 Why: Chaos is fundamental to understanding weather, orbits, and the limits of predictability in physics.
📡 Electrodynamics & PDEs
  • Laplace equation: finite difference + relaxation
  • Poisson equation: charge distributions → potentials
  • Diffusion equation: explicit vs. Crank-Nicolson
  • Wave equation: finite difference time domain (FDTD)
  • Boundary conditions: Dirichlet, Neumann, periodic
💡 Why: PDEs describe fields, heat flow, waves — nearly all of continuous physics. FDM is the gateway to PDE solving.
⚛️ Quantum Mechanics
  • TISE: shooting method, matrix diagonalization
  • Quantum harmonic oscillator eigenstates
  • Finite square well: bound states, tunneling
  • TDSE: Crank-Nicolson time evolution
  • Split-operator (FFT) method for TDSE
💡 Why: Quantum simulations are essential for modern physics. The split-operator method is used in real research.

Phase 3: Monte Carlo Universe

Master stochastic methods from basic integration to critical phenomena

⏱️ 6–10 weeks ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Advanced

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Implement and test random number generators
  • Compute integrals via Monte Carlo (error ~ 1/√N)
  • Implement Metropolis-Hastings algorithm
  • Simulate the 2D Ising model and locate T_c
  • Perform finite-size scaling analysis
🎰 Level 1: Monte Carlo Basics
  • RNG quality: period, uniformity tests, Mersenne Twister
  • CLT demonstration via sampling
  • Monte Carlo integration: estimating π, multidimensional integrals
  • Error scaling: σ ~ 1/√N (independent of dimension!)
  • Importance sampling basics
💡 Why: MC integration beats grid methods in high dimensions. This is how lattice QCD and financial models work.
🧲 Level 2: Metropolis & Ising Model
  • Markov chains: transition matrices, stationary distributions
  • Detailed balance: π(i)P(i→j) = π(j)P(j→i)
  • Metropolis algorithm: accept/reject with Boltzmann weight
  • 2D Ising model: spins, energy, magnetization
  • Measuring: ⟨M⟩, ⟨E⟩, χ (susceptibility), C (specific heat)
  • Critical temperature: T_c ≈ 2.269 J/k_B (Onsager)
💡 Why: The Ising model is the fruit fly of statistical mechanics. It teaches phase transitions, critical phenomena, and universality.
🔬 Level 3: Advanced MC Methods
  • Wolff cluster algorithm (flips clusters, reduces critical slowing down)
  • Autocorrelation time τ: effective sample size N_eff = N/(2τ)
  • Error estimation: jackknife, binning analysis
  • Finite-size scaling: T_c(L), χ_max(L) ~ L^(γ/ν)
  • Critical exponents: β, γ, ν (2D Ising exact values)
💡 Why: Near T_c, Metropolis has τ ~ L^z (z≈2.2). Wolff reduces z to ~0.25. Finite-size scaling extracts infinite-system exponents from finite simulations.

Phase 4: Advanced Frontiers

Molecular dynamics, machine learning, and high-performance computing

⏱️ 8–12+ weeks ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Expert

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Build a molecular dynamics simulation with periodic boundary conditions
  • Compute radial distribution function g(r) and diffusion coefficients
  • Implement physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)
  • Use Numba JIT for 10-100x Python speedups
  • Understand parallel computing basics (OpenMP, MPI, CUDA)
🔬 Molecular Dynamics
  • Velocity Verlet for MD (positions + velocities)
  • Lennard-Jones potential: V(r) = 4ε[(σ/r)¹² - (σ/r)⁶]
  • Periodic boundary conditions (PBC) & minimum image convention
  • Neighbor lists (Verlet list) for O(N) force computation
  • Thermostats: Berendsen, Nosé-Hoover (NVT ensemble)
  • Measurements: g(r), MSD, diffusion coefficient D
💡 Why: MD is the workhorse of condensed matter, biophysics, and materials science. g(r) reveals liquid structure; D connects to transport.
🤖 Machine Learning for Physics
  • Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs): physics as loss function
  • Solving PDEs with neural networks
  • Symbolic regression: discovering equations from data
  • Neural network potentials for MD
  • Phase detection with classification
💡 Why: ML is transforming computational physics. PINNs can solve PDEs without meshes. Symbolic regression can rediscover physical laws.
⚡ High-Performance Computing
  • Numba JIT: @njit decorator (10-100x speedup)
  • Profiling: cProfile, line_profiler, identify bottlenecks
  • OpenMP: shared-memory parallelism (threading)
  • MPI: distributed-memory parallelism (multiple nodes)
  • CUDA basics: GPU computing with CuPy or numba.cuda
  • When to use what: CPU vs GPU, threading vs MPI
💡 Why: Real research simulations run on clusters. Knowing HPC basics makes you immediately useful in a research group.

🐍 Learn to Code: Python from Scratch

Never coded before? Start here. Python is the best first language for computational physics — readable, powerful, and has the best scientific ecosystem in the world.

🏆
Why Python for Physics? Python reads almost like English pseudocode, so you can focus on the physics, not the syntax. NumPy performs millions of math operations per second. The entire scientific Python ecosystem (NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib, Pandas, SymPy) is free, open-source, and used in every physics department worldwide.

📖 Core Python Concepts — Detailed Explanations

1️⃣ Variables & Data Types

A variable is a named box that holds a value. Python figures out the type automatically.

# Integers (whole numbers) age = 21 particles = 1000 # Floats (decimals) — use these for physics! mass = 9.10938e-31 # Electron mass in kg gravity = 9.81 # m/s² pi = 3.14159265358979 # Strings (text) name = "Electron" element = 'Hydrogen' # Booleans (True/False) is_stable = True converged = False # Check the type of any variable print(type(mass)) # → <class 'float'> print(type(age)) # → <class 'int'>
Physics tip: Always use floats for physical quantities. Writing 1 instead of 1.0 in integer division can give you silent wrong answers!

2️⃣ Arithmetic & Math Operations

Python handles all standard arithmetic. The ** operator is for powers (very common in physics).

# Basic arithmetic x = 5 + 3 # → 8 (addition) y = 10 - 4 # → 6 (subtraction) z = 3 * 7 # → 21 (multiplication) q = 15 / 4 # → 3.75 (division — always float!) r = 15 // 4 # → 3 (integer/floor division) m = 15 % 4 # → 3 (modulo/remainder) p = 2 ** 10 # → 1024 (power: 2^10) # Physics examples import math E = 0.5 * 9.109e-31 * (3e6)**2 # KE = ½mv² F = 6.674e-11 * 5.97e24 * 70 / (6.37e6)**2 # F = GMm/r² c = math.sqrt(9 + 16) # Pythagoras: √(3²+4²) = 5 omega = 2 * math.pi * 60 # Angular frequency (60 Hz) print(f"KE = {E:.4e} J") print(f"F = {F:.2f} N") print(f"omega = {omega:.3f} rad/s")
Key operator: ** for exponentiation is used everywhere — r**2, v**3, N**(1/3). Scientific notation: 1.6e-19 means 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹.

3️⃣ Lists & Arrays

Lists store multiple values. In physics, we almost always upgrade to NumPy arrays for speed.

# Python list (flexible but slow) positions = [0.0, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0] velocities = [0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0] # Access elements (0-indexed!) print(positions[0]) # → 0.0 (first) print(positions[-1]) # → 4.0 (last) print(positions[1:3]) # → [1.0, 2.0] (slice) # Append to list positions.append(5.0) # NumPy array (fast — use for physics!) import numpy as np x = np.array([0.0, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0]) v = np.array([0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0]) # NumPy does VECTORIZED math (no loop needed!) KE = 0.5 * 1.0 * v**2 # Computes all at once! print(KE) # → [0. 0.125 0.5 1.125 2. ] # Create ranges of values t = np.linspace(0, 10, 1000) # 1000 points 0→10 x = np.arange(0, 5, 0.1) # 0.0, 0.1, 0.2, ...
Critical rule: NEVER loop over arrays for math. for i in range(len(v)): KE[i] = 0.5*v[i]**2 is 100× slower than KE = 0.5*v**2. Always vectorize!

4️⃣ If / Else (Conditionals)

Make decisions in your code. The indentation (4 spaces) is mandatory in Python — it defines code blocks.

# Basic if/else temperature = 2.5 # In units of J/k_B if temperature > 2.269: print("Above T_c: disordered (paramagnetic)") elif temperature == 2.269: print("AT the critical temperature!") else: print("Below T_c: ordered (ferromagnetic)") # Comparison operators # == (equal), != (not equal) # > (greater), < (less) # >= (greater or equal), <= (less or equal) # Combining conditions speed = 250000 # m/s is_relativistic = speed > 0.1 * 3e8 # 10% of light speed needs_correction = speed > 1e6 and is_relativistic if needs_correction: print("Use special relativity!") # Ternary (one-liner) label = "fast" if speed > 1e6 else "slow"
Physics use: Conditionals appear in accept/reject (Metropolis algorithm), boundary condition checks, convergence tests, and error handling in simulations.

5️⃣ Loops (for & while)

Repeat operations. Use for for known iterations, while until a condition is met.

import numpy as np # for loop over a range total_energy = 0.0 for i in range(100): # i = 0, 1, 2, ..., 99 total_energy += i * 0.1 # for loop over a list/array spins = [1, -1, 1, 1, -1, 1] magnetization = 0 for s in spins: magnetization += s print(f"M = {magnetization}") # enumerate: get index AND value for i, s in enumerate(spins): print(f"Spin {i}: {s}") # while loop (use for convergence) error = 1.0 tolerance = 1e-10 iteration = 0 while error > tolerance: new_value = 0.5 * (error + 2.0 / error) # Newton's method for √2 error = abs(new_value - error) iteration += 1 print(f"√2 ≈ {new_value:.12f} ({iteration} iterations)") # break / continue for n in range(100): if n == 50: break # stop the loop if n % 2 == 0: continue # skip even numbers
In computational physics: Loops drive time evolution (Euler, RK4), Metropolis sweeps, convergence iterations, and N-body force calculations. Keep inner loops as NumPy where possible.

6️⃣ Functions (def)

Reusable blocks of code. Good physics code consists of many small, well-named functions — one per concept.

import numpy as np # Basic function def kinetic_energy(mass, velocity): """Compute classical kinetic energy KE = ½mv²""" return 0.5 * mass * velocity**2 # Works on scalars AND NumPy arrays KE = kinetic_energy(9.1e-31, 1e6) # single electron KE_all = kinetic_energy(1.0, np.array([1, 2, 3, 4, 5])) # Multiple return values (returns a tuple) def euler_step(x, v, a, dt): """Single Euler integration step""" x_new = x + v * dt v_new = v + a * dt return x_new, v_new # Use it in a simulation x, v = 0.0, 10.0 # initial position, velocity dt = 0.01 # time step for _ in range(100): a = -9.81 # gravity x, v = euler_step(x, v, a, dt) # Default arguments def rk4(f, t, y, dt, *args): """4th-order Runge-Kutta integrator""" k1 = f(t, y, *args) k2 = f(t + dt/2, y + dt*k1/2, *args) k3 = f(t + dt/2, y + dt*k2/2, *args) k4 = f(t + dt, y + dt*k3, *args) return y + (dt/6) * (k1 + 2*k2 + 2*k3 + k4)
Best practice: Write a docstring (the triple-quoted string) for every function. Describe what it does, the inputs, and the units. Future-you will thank present-you.

7️⃣ NumPy Essentials

NumPy is the foundation of all scientific Python. Master these operations — you'll use them in every single simulation.

import numpy as np # Creating arrays zeros = np.zeros(100) # 100 zeros ones = np.ones((3, 3)) # 3×3 matrix of 1s eye = np.eye(4) # 4×4 identity matrix rand = np.random.rand(5, 5) # random 5×5 (0 to 1) t = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 1000) # 1000 evenly spaced points # Slicing 2D arrays (matrices) A = np.ones((5, 5)) A[0, :] # First row A[:, 2] # Third column (index 2) A[1:3, 1:3] # 2×2 submatrix # Math operations (all vectorized!) x = np.linspace(-5, 5, 1000) y = np.sin(x) * np.exp(-x**2/4) # Gaussian-modulated sine norm = np.sqrt(np.sum(y**2)) # L2 norm # Solving Ax = b A = np.array([[2., 1.], [1., 3.]]) b = np.array([5., 10.]) x = np.linalg.solve(A, b) # Eigenvalues (QM!) eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eig(A) # FFT (frequency analysis, split-operator QM) signal = np.sin(2*np.pi*60*t/t[-1]) # 60 Hz sine spectrum = np.fft.rfft(signal) freqs = np.fft.rfftfreq(len(t), d=t[1]-t[0])
Speed fact: np.linalg.solve(A, b) for a 1000×1000 system takes ~10ms. A pure Python loop would take minutes. NumPy calls optimized BLAS/LAPACK libraries written in Fortran.

8️⃣ Matplotlib Plotting

Visualization is science communication. A well-made plot tells a story. Learn the fundamentals here.

import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt # Always use subplots — more flexible fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 5)) # Left plot: sine wave t = np.linspace(0, 4*np.pi, 500) y = np.exp(-0.1*t) * np.sin(t) ax1 = axes[0] ax1.plot(t, y, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='Damped sine') ax1.axhline(y=0, color='k', linewidth=0.5) ax1.set_xlabel('Time (s)', fontsize=12) ax1.set_ylabel('Amplitude', fontsize=12) ax1.set_title('Damped Oscillator', fontsize=14) ax1.legend(fontsize=11) ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3) # Right plot: scatter (e.g., particle positions) N = 200 x = np.random.randn(N) y2 = np.random.randn(N) colors = np.sqrt(x**2 + y2**2) # color by radius ax2 = axes[1] sc = ax2.scatter(x, y2, c=colors, cmap='viridis', alpha=0.7, s=30) fig.colorbar(sc, ax=ax2, label='Radius') ax2.set_xlabel('x', fontsize=12) ax2.set_ylabel('y', fontsize=12) ax2.set_title('Particle Positions', fontsize=14) ax2.set_aspect('equal') plt.tight_layout() plt.savefig('my_plot.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight') plt.show()

🚫 10 Most Common Beginner Mistakes (and how to fix them)

1
Using for-loops instead of NumPy vectorization — for i in range(len(x)): y[i] = x[i]**2 → Replace with y = x**2. Vectorization is 50-500× faster.
2
Integer division giving wrong answers — 1/2 gives 0.5 in Python 3, but always write 1.0/2.0 for physics to be safe.
3
Not checking array shapes — Mismatched shapes cause cryptic errors. Always print(array.shape) when debugging.
4
Hardcoding "magic numbers" — Write G = 6.674e-11 at the top, not 6.674e-11 scattered throughout your code. Make constants named variables.
5
Too large a time step — Always test convergence: run with dt and dt/2. Results should be similar. If they differ a lot, your dt is too large.
6
Not plotting intermediate results — If your simulation gives NaN after 1000 steps, plot every 10 steps to find when it broke. Visualize early and often.
7
Copy-pasting code instead of writing functions — If you write the same calculation twice, make it a function. DRY = Don't Repeat Yourself.
8
Not tracking physical units — Always comment your units: v = 1.0 # m/s, m = 9.1e-31 # kg. A missing factor of 1000 (m vs mm) causes disasters.
9
Never using version control — git init every project. Commit after each milestone. "final_final_v3.py" is not version control.
10
Skipping error analysis — Numerical results without error estimates are incomplete. Always compute: how does your answer change with halved dt, doubled grid size, doubled N?

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about learning computational physics

How much time do I need per week? +

Aim for 10-15 hours per week for steady progress. This breaks down to: 3-4 hours reading/watching lectures, 4-5 hours coding projects, 2-3 hours reviewing math. You can go faster (20+ hrs/week) or slower (5-8 hrs/week) — the roadmap adapts to your pace. Consistency matters more than intensity.

What are the prerequisites? +

Minimal prerequisites: High school math (algebra, basic trigonometry) and willingness to learn. Phase 0 teaches you the calculus and linear algebra you need. Ideal prerequisites: One semester of calculus and introductory physics. If you've taken AP Calculus and AP Physics, you're perfectly positioned. No programming experience needed — we teach Python from scratch.

What career opportunities does this lead to? +

Computational physics skills are highly valued in: Research (academia, national labs like CERN, Fermilab, Los Alamos), Quantitative finance (Monte Carlo methods for derivatives pricing), Data science & ML (numerical methods, optimization), Software engineering (scientific computing, HPC), Engineering simulation (CFD, FEA at companies like ANSYS, SpaceX). The combination of physics intuition + coding + math is rare and valuable.

Do I need a powerful computer? +

No! Any modern laptop (8GB+ RAM) can handle Phases 0-4. The most demanding project (1000-atom MD) runs in seconds with Numba. For Phase 4 HPC topics, you can use free cloud resources: Google Colab (free GPUs), university clusters, or AWS free tier. You don't need a GPU until you start ML work, and even then, Colab provides free access.

Python vs. C++ vs. Julia — which language? +

Start with Python. It has the best ecosystem (NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib), is easiest to learn, and is used in most research groups. For performance-critical code, use Numba (JIT compilation gives C-like speed). Learn C++ later if you need maximum performance (large-scale MD, lattice QCD). Julia is promising but has a smaller community. Python + Numba covers 95% of use cases.

How do I know if I'm making progress? +

Use the readiness checklists at the end of each phase — they're your gate to the next level. Additionally: (1) Can you explain your project to a friend? (2) Can you read a computational physics paper and understand the methods section? (3) Can you debug a simulation that gives wrong results? If yes to all three, you're progressing well. The progress tracker on this page also helps visualize your journey.

Should I do all projects or pick some? +

Do at least the main project for each phase (falling ball, N-body, wave packet, Ising, MD, analysis pipeline). These are the minimum to build competence. The additional projects (g(r), diffusion, Wolff comparison, PINN) are for deeper mastery. If you're short on time, prioritize: Phase 3 Ising model > Phase 4 MD > Phase 2 wave packet. These three alone make a strong portfolio.

Is this roadmap suitable for self-study? +

Yes, absolutely. Every resource listed is available online. The roadmap is specifically designed for self-learners with structured phases, clear objectives, and validation checklists. However, joining a community (Physics Stack Exchange, Reddit r/computationalphysics) helps when you get stuck. If possible, find a study partner or mentor. Teaching someone else is the best way to solidify your understanding.

What if I've never coded at all? +

Start with the "Learn to Code" section on this page before beginning Phase 0. Spend 2–4 weeks going through the Python basics and doing the beginner exercises. Free resources like Harvard CS50P, Kaggle Learn, and Corey Schafer's YouTube channel will get you from zero to Python-comfortable in a month. Don't let "I can't code" stop you — the syntax is learnable in days; the physics thinking takes longer and is what this roadmap is really teaching.

How do I avoid getting stuck and giving up? +

The most common reason people quit is feeling stuck for too long. Three rules: (1) The 20-minute rule: if you're stuck on something for more than 20 minutes, Google it or ask on Stack Overflow. (2) Don't skip projects: coding the projects is where real learning happens — reading is not enough. (3) Make it visual: always plot your results. Seeing your simulation work is extremely motivating. Also: start small. Get a 2-particle N-body working before scaling to 8 planets.

What is the best order to learn the math? +

The optimal math order for computational physics is: (1) Calculus (derivatives, integrals) → (2) Linear algebra (matrices, eigenvalues) → (3) ODEs and their numerical solution → (4) PDEs basics → (5) Probability & statistics. Khan Academy handles 1–3 for free. 3Blue1Brown's "Essence" series gives deep geometric intuition for calculus and linear algebra. For PDEs: MIT OCW 18.03. Don't try to learn it all before coding — learn what you need for each phase.

Are there free certificates I can earn along the way? +

Yes! Harvard CS50P (edX, free audit + certificate option) after completing their Python course. Kaggle offers free completion certificates for all their learn courses (Python, NumPy, ML). Coursera allows financial aid for certificates if you can't afford them — apply and you'll likely get 100% off. However, your GitHub portfolio of working simulations is worth more to employers and grad programs than any certificate. Prioritize building projects over collecting badges.
